\begin{abstract}
Hidden states in fitted hidden Markov models are often interpreted as latent regimes. This interpretation can fail under emission misspecification. We formalize a sufficient mechanism under which an additional fitted state is a Kullback-Leibler correction to a restrictive emission family, not an additional scientific regime. The main result compares observed laws only: for a persistent aggregate HMM whose emissions are identifiable finite mixtures from a dominated family, a refined HMM reproduces the observed process exactly, whereas every non-refined HMM with one fitted-family emission per aggregate regime has strictly positive relative-entropy rate. A Gaussian simulation and an enriched-emission refit illustrate the diagnostic implication. A three-state Gaussian HMM splits a heterogeneous aggregate regime, while a two-state HMM with two-component Gaussian emissions absorbs the same structure inside the emission distribution. Thus the fitted number of states can measure emission approximation order rather than the number of substantive regimes.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} hidden Markov model; misspecification; finite mixtures; relative entropy rate; Kullback-Leibler projection; state splitting.

\section{Introduction}

Hidden Markov models (HMMs) are widely used to segment dependent observations into latent states. In applications, these states are often interpreted as behaviours, regimes, or classes. This interpretation is attractive but fragile: a fitted state is simultaneously a scientific label and a component of a statistical approximation.

The fragility is most visible under emission misspecification. Suppose the data-generating process has a small number of aggregate regimes, but the conditional distribution within one regime is not contained in the emission family used for fitting. Then a likelihood-based HMM can create extra states to approximate the shape of that misspecified emission. A heavy-tailed, overdispersed, skewed, or heterogeneous regime may be split into several fitted states, even when there is no additional aggregate behaviour.

This empirical difficulty is well documented in HMM order selection. In particular, \citet{PohleLangrockVanBeestSchmidt2017} show that standard information criteria can favour numbers of states that are difficult to interpret scientifically. One practical response is to enrich the observation model directly, for example with flexible nonparametric emissions as in \citet{LangrockKneibSohnDeRuiter2015}. Likelihood theory for HMMs also shows that, under misspecification, maximum likelihood targets pseudo-true parameters minimizing limiting KL or relative-entropy criteria; see \citet{Leroux1992,BickelRitovRyden1998,DoucMoulines2012}. General HMM inference is treated in \citet{CappeMoulinesRyden2005} and \citet{ZucchiniMacDonaldLangrock2016}. Our aim is to give a short structural mechanism explaining why a pseudo-true observed law can require more fitted states than the scientific number of regimes.

The main contribution is \Cref{thm:marginal}. It compares observed laws only. Under compactness, identifiable finite mixtures, pairwise distinct refined atoms, and uniformly positive aggregate transitions, a refined HMM reproduces the observed process exactly, whereas every non-refined HMM with the smaller number of states is bounded away from the truth in relative-entropy rate. The transition-positivity assumption excludes structural zero transitions; it is used as a transparent sufficient condition for a one-step predictive lower bound, not as a necessary condition. Two preliminary results prepare this theorem. Proposition~\ref{prop:aligned} is an aligned comparison, meaning that the hidden aggregate trajectory is fixed. Proposition~\ref{prop:refinement} records the classical state-space refinement behind mixture emissions, a construction closely related to standard HMM state augmentation \citep{Rabiner1989,ZucchiniMacDonaldLangrock2016}.

The result is deliberately stated for a general dominated emission family. It applies whenever the finite-mixture geometry is identifiable and compact. Classical finite-mixture identifiability goes back to \citet{Teicher1963} and \citet{YakowitzSpragins1968}; HMM identifiability and order estimation are studied by \citet{GassiatKeribin2000}, \citet{AllmanMatiasRhodes2009}, \citet{GassiatCleynenRobin2016}, and \citet{Lehericy2018}. The present paper uses mixture geometry directly through predictive densities. It should be read as a sufficient mechanism, not as a claim that every extra state is corrective.

\section{Notation and emission-mixture geometry}

Let $(\Yspace,\mathcal B,\lambda)$ be a dominated observation space. For densities $p,q$, write
\[
D(p\Vert q)=\int p(y)\log\{p(y)/q(y)\}\,\lambda(dy),
\]
with the usual conventions. We use Pinsker's inequality,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pinsker}
D(p\Vert q)\geq \frac12\norm{p-q}_1^2.
\end{equation}
Let $\Fcal=\{f_\eta:\eta\in\Theta\}$ be the fitted emission family and define the class of mixtures of at most $K$ fitted-family emissions by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:MK}
\Mcal_K=\left\{\sum_{j=1}^K v_j f_{\eta_j}: v_j\geq0,\ \sum_{j=1}^K v_j=1,\ \eta_j\in\Theta\right\}.
\end{equation}
Zero weights are allowed, so $\Mcal_K\subseteq\Mcal_{K+1}$.

For $K\geq1$, let $\Hcal_K(\Fcal)$ denote the class of observed laws generated by $K$-state HMMs with arbitrary initial distribution, arbitrary transition matrix, and emission densities in $\Fcal$. Only the one-step predictive form of such laws is used below.

For stationary observed processes $P,Q$, define the lower relative-entropy rate
\[
\underline D^{\rm rate}(P\Vert Q)=\liminf_{T\to\infty}\frac1T D(P^{1:T}\Vert Q^{1:T}).
\]
The liminf is enough for the lower bounds below.

Fix an integer $K_0\geq1$. In the applications below, $K_0$ is the number of aggregate scientific regimes.

\begin{assumption}[Emission family]\label{ass:F}
The parameter space $\Theta$ is compact. The map $\eta\mapsto f_\eta$ is continuous from $\Theta$ to $L^1(\lambda)$, and $f_\eta>0$ $\lambda$-almost everywhere for every $\eta\in\Theta$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Identifiable finite mixtures]\label{ass:idmix}
Finite mixtures of $\Fcal$ have identifiable minimal order. If two finite mixtures with strictly positive weights and pairwise distinct parameters are equal $\lambda$-almost everywhere, then they have the same number of components and agree up to label swapping.
\end{assumption}

\begin{lemma}[Separation from lower-order mixtures]\label{lem:separation}
Assume Assumptions~\ref{ass:F} and~\ref{ass:idmix}. Let $\eta_1^0,\ldots,\eta_R^0$ be pairwise distinct, with $R>K_0$. For $c>0$ such that $Rc\leq1$, define
\[
\Acal_c=\left\{\sum_{r=1}^R v_r f_{\eta_r^0}: v_r\geq c,\ \sum_{r=1}^R v_r=1\right\}.
\]
Then
\[
d_c:=\inf_{p\in\Acal_c}\inf_{q\in\Mcal_{K_0}}\norm{p-q}_1>0,
\]
and hence
\[
\inf_{p\in\Acal_c}\inf_{q\in\Mcal_{K_0}}D(p\Vert q)\geq \frac12d_c^2>0.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By Assumption~\ref{ass:F}, $\Mcal_{K_0}$ is the continuous image of the compact set $\Delta_{K_0}\times\Theta^{K_0}$, hence compact in $L^1$. The set $\Acal_c$ is also compact. The two sets are disjoint: if $p\in\Acal_c\cap\Mcal_{K_0}$, then a mixture with exactly $R$ positive-weight, pairwise distinct components equals a mixture whose zero-weight and repeated-atom components can be reduced to a minimal representation with at most $K_0$ components. This contradicts identifiable minimal order. Two disjoint compact sets in a metric space have positive distance. The KL bound follows from \eqref{eq:pinsker}.
\end{proof}

\section{Aligned KL correction by state splitting}

Let $Z_t\in\{1,\ldots,K_0\}$ be a stationary aggregate Markov chain with transition matrix $\Gamma^0$ and stationary distribution $\pi$, with $\pi_k>0$. Suppose
\[
Y_t\mid Z_t=k\sim g_k.
\]
For $m\geq1$, define
\[
\delta_k(m)=\inf_{q\in\Mcal_m}D(g_k\Vert q).
\]

\begin{proposition}[Aligned KL improvement]\label{prop:aligned}
If state $k$ is represented by one emission from $\Fcal$, the best aligned KL risk is
\[
\mathcal K_{\rm unref}=\sum_{k=1}^{K_0}\pi_k\delta_k(1).
\]
If state $k$ is refined into $m_k$ substates with emissions from $\Fcal$, the best aligned KL risk is
\[
\mathcal K_{\rm ref}=\sum_{k=1}^{K_0}\pi_k\delta_k(m_k).
\]
Consequently, if $\delta_{k^\star}(m_{k^\star})<\delta_{k^\star}(1)$ for some $k^\star$, then $\mathcal K_{\rm ref}<\mathcal K_{\rm unref}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In the aligned comparison, the hidden trajectory law is fixed at the true aggregate chain. Thus all transition terms cancel in the complete-data likelihood ratio. With one fitted emission $f_{\eta_k}$ per aggregate state, stationarity gives risk
\[
\sum_{k=1}^{K_0}\pi_kD(g_k\Vert f_{\eta_k}),
\]
and taking the infimum over $\eta_1,\ldots,\eta_{K_0}$ gives $\sum_k\pi_k\delta_k(1)$. With $m_k$ substates inside aggregate state $k$, the aggregate fitted emission is any $q_k\in\Mcal_{m_k}$; taking the infimum over these mixtures gives $\sum_k\pi_k\delta_k(m_k)$. Since $\Mcal_1\subseteq\Mcal_{m_k}$, all terms weakly decrease, and one positive-weight strict decrease yields the claim.
\end{proof}

\section{Exact refinement with persistent aggregate dynamics}

The next construction is the standard state-augmentation device used here to preserve the aggregate dynamics exactly.

\begin{proposition}[Exact HMM refinement]\label{prop:refinement}
Suppose the aggregate emissions are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gkmixture}
g_k(y)=\sum_{a=1}^{m_k}w_{k,a}f_{\eta^0_{k,a}}(y),\qquad w_{k,a}>0,\quad \sum_a w_{k,a}=1.
\end{equation}
Let the refined states be $(k,a)$ and define $r(k,a)=k$. Set
\begin{equation}\label{eq:reftransition}
\Gamma^{\rm ref}_{(k,a),(\ell,b)}=\gamma^0_{k\ell}w_{\ell,b},
\end{equation}
and let $Y_t\mid X_t=(k,a)\sim f_{\eta^0_{k,a}}$. If $X_1$ has distribution $\pi_k w_{k,a}$ on $(k,a)$, then the observed law of the refined HMM is exactly the observed law of the aggregate HMM with transitions $\Gamma^0$ and emissions $g_k$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The matrix in \eqref{eq:reftransition} is stochastic since $\sum_{\ell,b}\gamma^0_{k\ell}w_{\ell,b}=1$. The distribution $\pi_k w_{k,a}$ is stationary because
\[
\sum_{k,a}\pi_k w_{k,a}\gamma^0_{k\ell}w_{\ell,b}=w_{\ell,b}\sum_k\pi_k\gamma^0_{k\ell}=\pi_\ell w_{\ell,b}.
\]
Moreover,
\[
\Pbb\{r(X_t)=\ell\mid X_{t-1}=(k,a)\}=\sum_b\gamma^0_{k\ell}w_{\ell,b}=\gamma^0_{k\ell}.
\]
Finally, summing over all substate paths compatible with a fixed aggregate path $z_{1:T}$ gives
\[
\pi_{z_1}\prod_{t=2}^T\gamma^0_{z_{t-1},z_t}\prod_{t=1}^T\sum_{a=1}^{m_{z_t}}w_{z_t,a}f_{\eta^0_{z_t,a}}(y_t)
=
\pi_{z_1}\prod_{t=2}^T\gamma^0_{z_{t-1},z_t}\prod_{t=1}^Tg_{z_t}(y_t).
\]
Summing over aggregate paths proves equality of all observed finite-dimensional laws.
\end{proof}

\section{Persistent marginal theorem}

Let the aggregate transition matrix satisfy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mintrans}
\varepsilon:=\min_{k,\ell}\gamma^0_{k\ell}>0.
\end{equation}
This permits persistent regimes with large diagonal probabilities, while keeping every aggregate state present in every one-step prediction. Let
\[
R=K_{\rm ref}:=\sum_{k=1}^{K_0}m_k,
\qquad
w_{\min}:=\min_{k,a}w_{k,a},
\qquad
c:=\varepsilon w_{\min}.
\]
Assume $R>K_0$ and the refined atoms $\eta^0_{k,a}$ are pairwise distinct.
For this value of $c$, the condition $Rc\leq1$ required in Lemma~\ref{lem:separation} is automatic, because the $R$ refined predictive weights are all at least $c$ and sum to one.

\begin{theorem}[Persistent marginal KL separation]\label{thm:marginal}
Under Assumptions~\ref{ass:F} and~\ref{ass:idmix}, the aggregate HMM with transitions $\Gamma^0$ satisfying \eqref{eq:mintrans} and emissions \eqref{eq:gkmixture} satisfies
\[
\inf_{Q\in\Hcal_{K_{\rm ref}}(\Fcal)}\underline D^{\rm rate}(P_0^Y\Vert Q)=0,
\]
but every $K_0$-state HMM with emissions in $\Fcal$ satisfies
\[
\underline D^{\rm rate}(P_0^Y\Vert Q)\geq \frac12d_c^2>0,
\]
where $d_c$ is the separation constant from Lemma~\ref{lem:separation}. Hence
\[
\inf_{Q\in\Hcal_{K_{\rm ref}}(\Fcal)}\underline D^{\rm rate}(P_0^Y\Vert Q)
<
\inf_{Q\in\Hcal_{K_0}(\Fcal)}\underline D^{\rm rate}(P_0^Y\Vert Q).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The first claim follows from Proposition~\ref{prop:refinement}: the refined $K_{\rm ref}$-state HMM reproduces the observed law exactly.

Now fix any candidate $K_0$-state HMM with emissions $f_{\eta_1},\ldots,f_{\eta_{K_0}}$. Its one-step predictive density has the form
\[
p_t^Q(y\mid y_{1:t-1})=\sum_{j=1}^{K_0}\lambda_{t,j}(y_{1:t-1})f_{\eta_j}(y),
\]
so $p_t^Q(\cdot\mid y_{1:t-1})\in\Mcal_{K_0}$ for every history.

For the true aggregate HMM, let
\[
\alpha_{t,k}(y_{1:t-1})=\Pbb_0(Z_t=k\mid Y_{1:t-1}=y_{1:t-1}).
\]
Stationarity and \eqref{eq:mintrans} imply $\alpha_{1,k}=\pi_k\geq\varepsilon$. For $t\geq2$,
\[
\alpha_{t,\ell}(y_{1:t-1})=
\sum_k \Pbb_0(Z_{t-1}=k\mid Y_{1:t-1}=y_{1:t-1})\gamma^0_{k\ell}
\geq \varepsilon.
\]
Therefore the true predictive density is
\[
p_t^0(y\mid Y_{1:t-1})=
\sum_{k,a}\alpha_{t,k}(Y_{1:t-1})w_{k,a}f_{\eta^0_{k,a}}(y),
\]
with every refined weight at least $c=\varepsilon w_{\min}$. Thus $p_t^0(\cdot\mid Y_{1:t-1})\in\Acal_c$ almost surely. By Lemma~\ref{lem:separation},
\[
D\{p_t^0(\cdot\mid Y_{1:t-1})\Vert p_t^Q(\cdot\mid Y_{1:t-1})\}\geq \frac12d_c^2
\]
almost surely. If any conditional divergence is infinite, the desired bound is immediate. Otherwise, the chain rule for relative entropy yields
\[
D(P_0^{Y_{1:T}}\Vert Q^{Y_{1:T}})=
\sum_{t=1}^T\Ebb_0\left[D\{p_t^0(\cdot\mid Y_{1:t-1})\Vert p_t^Q(\cdot\mid Y_{1:t-1})\}\right]
\geq T\frac12d_c^2.
\]
Divide by $T$ and take the liminf.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[On the transition positivity assumption]
The assumption $\min_{k,\ell}\gamma^0_{k\ell}>0$ is sufficient, not necessary. It gives a short one-step predictive proof and an explicit lower bound. A more abstract replacement is possible: compactness of a regular $K_0$-state class, lower semicontinuity of the entropy-rate map, observed-law identifiability, and non-representability of at least one aggregate emission by $\Fcal$ imply a positive separation by contradiction. That route is less transparent and is not needed for the present sufficient mechanism.
\end{remark}

\section{Numerical illustration}

We simulated a persistent two-regime aggregate HMM with
\[
\Gamma^0=\begin{pmatrix}0.93&0.07\\0.05&0.95\end{pmatrix}.
\]
The first aggregate regime had a two-component Gaussian mixture emission, $0.70N(-1.20,0.55^2)+0.30N(1.10,0.45^2)$, and the second regime had emission $N(3.80,0.65^2)$. We fitted a Gaussian-emission HMM with $K=2$, a Gaussian-emission HMM with $K=3$, and an enriched $K=2$ HMM with two Gaussian components per state. \section{Discussion}

The results show that, under transparent sufficient conditions,
\[
K_{\rm fitted}=K_{\rm aggregate}+\text{emission-correction substates}
\]
can be the correct likelihood geometry. Extra states need not be spurious, but neither should they automatically be interpreted as new scientific regimes. They may be necessary to approximate a misspecified emission family.

The theory suggests a diagnostic workflow. After fitting a model with additional states, examine whether the extra state disappears when the emission family is enriched directly, for example by a heavy-tailed, overdispersed, copula, or explicit finite-mixture emission. If it does, the state was likely corrective. This distinction is relevant for likelihood-based state selection and for applied HMM interpretation, especially in domains where states are routinely assigned substantive meaning.
